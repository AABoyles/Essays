Modeling Global Population Dynamics

System dynamic modeling is a methodology in which values are defined by the causal relationships between variables. These values can then be tested against empirical records, and used to forecast future effects. This sort of modeling works very well with anything that can be defined in terms of population counts. The global human population model is simple application, having only a single population (that is, humans), and a fixed number of means by which that number can change. This study employs the data collected by the World Bank in the World Development Indices to complete this picture. To start with, consider birthrate. Over the 49 year period from 1960-2009, the value drops from 4.91 to 2.52. That’s a change of 2.39, or a mean change of .049 annually. But what does this number actually mean? The Word Development Indicator explanation reads, “The average number of births per woman.” This definition creates two new problems. This first is that the number of living people alive at any given time is given, but the number of women is not. The second is much more subtle: this value is evaluated over steps of time (in this case, years), but the rate is measured over a woman’s lifespan. Despite outperforming the System Dynamic model over the data available, the linear projection fails on two fronts: first, if we use it to infer the global population backwards in time, we find that humanity first walked the Earth around 1920 C.E., clearly an absurd proposition. Secondly, it is equally uninformative about the future state of the human population.

In lieu of comparing the system dynamic model with one which makes no sense outside of a narrow time horizon, other data must be employed against which to compare. The U.N. publishes a biannual prediction of the global population trajectory. The U.N. employs three creatively-named models: “high,” “medium,” and “low.” High is a worst-case scenario. It’s basically a prediction that Malthus was right, and populations grow exponentially. This outcome would be inconvenient–lots of starvation, poor sanitation, and generally bad conditions with few per capita resources to improve upon them. Medium is much sunnier–it represents the world in which we want to live. Around the year 2050, the human population stabilizes near 9 billion. Low represents the unknown unknowns: war, famine, disease, disaster ravage the human population in the next 40 years.

Observe nearly-uniform, fanning-out effect. As time progresses, the predictions of the model diverge sharply from each other. Only the low model begins to fall, while the medium converges on 9 billion, and the top three forecasts (System Dynamic, Linear, and the UN’s High, respectively in increasing order) continue to increase at varying speeds. In 2004 the UN published projections out to 2300, against which the other models may be further validated.

Low and Medium have both held their positions for centuries, and it looks like the System Dynamic model approaches a point of convergence, but linear keeps growing and high is skyrocketing.

There are important sources of distinction between my model and the U.N.’s. The UN possesses an immediate advantage in that its models may start projecting based on data which is empirically accurate for the current year. Because my model was calibrated on the data from the prior 50 years, I must start with the year 1960 and project to 2010 and beyond. This means the number I have for this year’s population is probably a little inflated, so the model on the whole is relatively high. The other is that my model continues to project expectations into the realm of unlikelihood–for example, by 2300, the System Dynamic model suggests the mean female lifespan should be over 100, and still rising.

This brings us to a terminal question: will this model indicate that the global population converges? To start, we must collapse the simultaneous equations of the model into a single function. This is possible because each equation in the model is contingent only upon other values in the system (none of which are feedback process except the canonical Births and Deaths), time (represented as the variable n), or the dependent variable, GlobalPop. So the final function should show the relationship between time and Global Population.

Both the expressions representing Births and Deaths converge on 0, so the equilibrium state exists. How big is it? How long will it take? Equilibrium is the point at which the global population no longer changes from year to year, or if it does, it begins to do so cyclically. There aren’t any cyclical parameters in the model, and there aren’t enough independent variables for cyclicality to emerge organically, so a single point of convergence is the most likely outcome in this case. There are three approaches to solving this system: Assuming the above is true, all cases of GlobalPop at any time other than may be substituted for GlobalPop at time n, and the resultant polynomial function may be solved. Alternately, the Calculus may be employed, and the salient equation differentiated to identify whatever minima or maxima might induce a stable conclusion. Finally, a simple simulation may be run over an arbitrarily large number of years (or steps) to identify the same minima or maxima. Because this system is fundamentally deterministic (the model, not the modeled phenomena), this need only be done once: every run of this simulation will produce exactly the same results.

The global population should reach a maxima sometime in the 23rd century. But this is not a equilibrium: it’s a tipping point. Someday, birthrates will have slowed down more than deathrates, so that even though people live longer (in excess of 1700 years by the end), they procreate so infrequently that the human species dies off relatively quickly. This brings that information derived from the earlier limit approach into sharp relief: If no one is being born, and no one is dying, that’s either because humans have effectively become impotent immortals, or there aren’t any humans left. The model indicates the latter is the final fate of humanity.

There are a few sources of error in this assessment, which should gives pause before considering these conclusions with any seriousness:

The life expectancy is latent–it describes the life expectancy of people born in that year, not the life expectancy of people living in that year. We didn’t describe the female percentage of the population with a function, which makes it inflexible.

The linear and exponential models we use to generate many of the coefficients for the equations of the final model are imperfect.

The data are imperfect: the World Development Indicators are calculated using a completely different methodology, which one hopes would be fairly accurate. Even so, it would be logistically impossible to count every single person on the planet at a fixed point in time–people are born and die every minute. The WDIs are correct to the ballpark (as are the models we devise from them), but only so.

Even so, this model presents a solid, nontrivial foundation for System Dynamic Modeling based upon, and validated against, empirical data. System Dynamic modeling represents the causal linkages between variables, thereby creating much more compelling forecasts. The divergence of model results from reality indicate the absence of other critical causal factors in the model or the overestimation of the effectiveness of some modeled causal pathway(s). I claim the results of this model could survive an exogenous shock to many of the variables represented, while the linear model lacks any flexibility to compensate for such exogenous shocks.
